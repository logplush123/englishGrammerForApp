ENGLISH GRAMMAR: PUNCTUATION

Punctuation Marks

**Punctuation marks** are essential tools used in writing to organize words, clauses, and sentences. They help convey meaning, emphasize points, and control the flow and rhythm of your writing. Here are the major punctuation marks in English:

A. Full Stop [.]

(नेपाली: पूर्णविराम)

The **Full Stop** (or Period) is used at the end of a declarative statement or command to indicate a complete stop. Always leave a single space after a full stop before starting the next sentence.

Example: She is my sister. She works in India.

B. Question Mark [?]

(नेपाली: प्रश्नवाचक चिन्ह)

The **Question Mark** is used at the end of a direct question. It replaces the full stop in interrogative sentences.

Example: Am I a farmer? (Correct)

C. Exclamation Mark [!]

(नेपाली: विस्मयादिबोधक चिन्ह)

The **Exclamation Mark** ends a sentence and is used to show strong emotion, such as shock, surprise, horror, excitement, or pleasure.

Example: Oh, what a beautiful flower!

D. Comma [,]

(नेपाली: अल्पविराम)

The **Comma** indicates a short pause in a sentence, separates items in a list, or sets off non-essential information, introductory phrases, or clauses.

Example: I bought apples, bananas, and oranges.

E. Colon [:]

(नेपाली: उपविराम)

The **Colon** is placed before a list, an explanation, or an example that directly follows a complete sentence.

Example: The Bible has two parts: Old Testament and New Testament.

F. Semicolon [;]

(नेपाली: अर्धविराम)

The **Semicolon** joins two independent clauses (complete sentences) that are closely related in meaning but not joined by a coordinating conjunction (like 'and', 'but', or 'so').

Example: I don't like the job; I am going to resign.

G. Quotation Marks [""]

(नेपाली: उद्धरण चिन्ह)

We use **Quotation Marks** (or Inverted Commas) to indicate a person's direct speech (the exact words spoken) or to quote a text.

Example: She said, "I'm very tired."

H. Apostrophe [']

(नेपाली: अपोस्ट्रोफी)

The **Apostrophe** is used in two ways: (1) to indicate possession or belonging (e.g., *Upama's pen*), and (2) to show contractions where letters have been omitted (e.g., *it's* for *it is* or *doesn't* for *does not*).

Example: It's Upama's pen. Its color isn't good.

Correct Answers

  1. You'd rather do what's very important only.
  2. Raman said, "I'll go by bus."
  3. **Rabin** said, "**Hurray! W**e've won the match."
  4. **Kiran didn't** like coffee**,** so **Ramesh** ordered **Gita** to bring tea for him.
  5. **Khilaraj Regmi,** also the **Chief Justice,** is the **Prime Minister** of **Nepal**.
  6. With much sweat on his face, he said, "**I'm** so tired, aren't **I**?"
  7. "If we walked quickly, **we'd** reach **Namche** in time," said **Krishna**.
  8. "Oh no," he cried. "I didn't do it, did I?" (Note: The original was a run-on; split for clarity)
  9. "Students, haven't you been to **Lumbini?**" the teacher said.

*The answers above include corrections for capitalization, internal commas, apostrophes, and splitting run-on sentences to follow standard English grammar conventions.
